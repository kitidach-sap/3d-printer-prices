Resin Technologies Compared

Not all resin printers work the same way. There are three main technologies available to consumers:

TechnologyHow It WorksDetail LevelSpeedPrice Range
MSLALCD masks UV light to cure entire layers at onceExcellent (25-50μm XY)Fast (cures full layer simultaneously)$150–$500
DLPDigital projector cures resin with focused lightVery good (35-75μm XY)Fast (similar to MSLA)$300–$800
SLALaser traces each layer point by pointOutstanding (25μm XY)Slow (draws each point individually)$1,500+

MSLA is the standard choice for most consumers. It offers the best balance of price, speed, and quality. Brands like ELEGOO, Anycubic, and Phrozen dominate this segment. DLP printers are less common in the consumer space but offer excellent results. SLA (laser-based) is mostly an industrial technology — Formlabs is the main consumer-friendly brand, but at significantly higher prices.

What About 4K and 8K Screens?

You'll see resin printers advertise "4K" or "8K" resolution. This refers to the LCD screen resolution, not print quality directly. Higher resolution means smaller individual pixels, which translates to finer XY detail. However, the relationship isn't linear — an 8K screen on a large build plate might have similar pixel density to a 4K screen on a smaller plate. Focus on XY resolution in micrometers (the smaller the number, the finer the detail) rather than marketing screen resolution numbers.

Key Specs That Matter

Build Volume

Resin printers have smaller build volumes than FDM. The most common sizes:

  • Small (120×68mm) — Perfect for miniatures, jewelry, and dental. Most affordable entry point.
  • Medium (192×120mm) — Versatile for various projects. Can batch-print many miniatures or print medium-sized figurines.
  • Large (218×123mm+) — For larger models, helmets, or very high-volume batch printing. More expensive machines.

Z-Resolution (Layer Height)

Most resin printers can print at 0.01–0.05mm layer heights. In practice, 0.05mm is the sweet spot for most applications — fast enough for reasonable print times while producing virtually invisible layer lines.

Light Source Lifespan

MSLA printers use LCD screens that degrade over time. Budget for screen replacement every 1,500–2,000 hours of printing. This is a running cost unique to resin printing that many buyers overlook. Check replacement screen prices before buying — some models charge $30, others charge $80+.

Safety Requirements

This is the most important section in this guide. Resin printing requires safety precautions that FDM does not:

Must-Have Safety Equipment

  • Nitrile gloves — Wear them every time you handle resin or uncured prints. Resin is a skin sensitizer; repeated exposure can cause permanent allergic reactions.
  • Ventilation — Either a well-ventilated room (open windows, fan) or an enclosure with carbon filtration. Resin fumes cause headaches and respiratory irritation.
  • Safety glasses — Protect against splashes when pouring or cleaning resin.
  • Dedicated workspace — Resin spills are difficult to clean and can damage surfaces. Use a silicone mat under your printer.

Storage and Disposal

Uncured resin is classified as hazardous. Never pour liquid resin or contaminated IPA down the drain. Cure waste resin under UV light before disposing of it as solid waste. Keep resin bottles and printers away from children and pets.

The Resin Printing Workflow

Understanding the workflow helps you decide if resin's extra steps are worth the detail improvement:

  1. Prepare the model — Import into slicer (Chitubox, Lychee, or UVtools), add supports, set layer height
  2. Fill the vat — Pour resin into the printer's resin vat (wearing gloves)
  3. Print — UV light cures resin layer by layer. Print times: 2-8 hours typically
  4. Remove print — Carefully detach from build plate (gloves on)
  5. Wash — Submerge in isopropyl alcohol for 3-5 minutes to remove uncured resin. Wash-and-cure stations automate this.
  6. UV cure — Expose to UV light for 5-15 minutes to fully harden the print
  7. Remove supports — Clip off support structures, sand if necessary
  8. Clean up — Filter resin back into bottle, wipe down workspace

Compared to FDM's simpler "print → remove" workflow, resin adds about 20-30 minutes of post-processing per print session.

Who Should (and Shouldn't) Buy Resin

✅ Buy Resin If:

  • You print tabletop miniatures, wargaming figures, or D&D models
  • You make jewelry prototypes or castable wax patterns
  • You need dental or medical models with high precision
  • You value surface finish over structural strength
  • You have a ventilated workspace away from living areas

❌ Don't Buy Resin If:

  • You want to print large functional parts (get FDM instead)
  • You don't have a ventilated workspace
  • You're uncomfortable handling chemicals
  • You want the simplest possible workflow
  • Budget is tight (FDM running costs are significantly lower)

Frequently Asked Questions

How long do resin prints last?

Properly cured resin prints can last for years. However, standard resin is brittle and can yellow with prolonged UV exposure (sunlight). For outdoor use or functional parts, choose ABS-like or engineering resin formulations.

Can I use any resin with any printer?

Most 405nm UV resin works with most MSLA printers. However, you'll need to adjust exposure settings for each resin brand and type. Always check the manufacturer's recommended settings as a starting point. Explore compatible materials with our Material Compatibility Explorer.

Is water-washable resin worth it?

Water-washable resin eliminates the need for IPA, which saves money and reduces chemical handling. However, it's slightly less detailed than standard resin and can develop surface tackiness in humid environments. It's a great option for beginners who want a simpler workflow.

How loud are resin printers?

Resin printers are very quiet — typically under 45 dB. The loudest part is the build plate lifting between layers. They're significantly quieter than most FDM printers.

How much resin does a miniature use?

A standard 28mm tabletop miniature uses approximately 5-10mL of resin, which costs about $0.15-$0.40 per figure. Even with supports and waste resin, you can print hundreds of miniatures from a single 1-liter bottle.
